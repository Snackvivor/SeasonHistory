As for myself, I am potato. At first, the spud is very unassuming. It's a root vegetable that as is isn't too good. But the potato has two very key things going for it: The first is it's versatility. The potato is able to be cut into a whole manner of ways, be it fries (french or german), hash browns (McD's or standard versions), mashed, baked, scalloped, sliced thin and crisped into chips, made into a salad, the list goes on and on. It is a vegetable that can do anything and be anything. The second thing is how the chef chooses to prepare it. With its versatility, it can fit into just about any dish, and so when the chef comes into the kitchen, the potato makes it known that it can adapt to be whatever they need for that day. The potato is the quintessential team player. It wants the best for others because it knows that what makes a good breakfast is more than the sum of its parts but how they work together. So, the potato not only brings out the best in itself with the right effort, but it also wants to bring out the best in others. The potato is a noble vegetable. It plays its part in scenes often much bigger than itself. But it always has the potential and the ability to shine above all the rest, to be loved or to be hated, to stand apart as the best or be shunned for not being the way people wanted it. At the end of the day, the story of the potato is what you choose to make of it. But I think that this potato has been a good one.
